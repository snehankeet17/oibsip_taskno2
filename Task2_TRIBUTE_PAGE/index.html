SWAMI VIVEKANANDA


“Strength is Life, Weakness is Death. Expansion is Life, Contraction is Death. Love is Life, Hatred is Death.”

― Swami Vivekananda

(12 January 1863 - 4 July 1902)

The Life of Swami Vivekananda

Swami Vivekananda was a prominent disciple of Sri Ramakrishna and a renowned spokesperson for Vedanta. He focused on teaching universal principles instead of personalities and his lectures, letters, and poems are compiled in The Complete Works of Swami Vivekananda. In 1893, he represented Hinduism at the World's Parliament of Religions in Chicago, where he gave a famous speech that led to invitations to speak all over America and Europe. Many Vedanta Societies established in America and Europe during the early 20th century can trace their origins directly to Vivekananda or those who heard him speak during this time.

Vivekananda founded the Ramakrishna Order in India in 1898, and he was accompanied on his return to the United States by his brother monk, Swami Turiyananda. Other disciples of Sri Ramakrishna, such as Swamis Saradananda and Abhedananda, also taught widely. Vivekananda was a spiritual leader with a strong presence and an impressive intellect, and he was an active teacher and writer. He wrote poems and hymns in Bengali, English, and Sanskrit, some of which are still sung in Vedanta centers around the world. Vivekananda was ahead of his time in advocating for women and Westerners to practice Vedanta and to be leaders in the field. For example, he initiated Sara Ellen Waldo and Margaret Noble, later known as Sister Nivedita, as sannyasinis and brahmacharinis, respectively, which was a radical act for the time. Sister Nivedita devoted her life to both Vedanta and the education of Indian girls, and her book The Master as I Saw Him provides an account of the years she spent with Swamiji.

Vivekananda's main teaching, both in the West and in India, was Practical Vedanta. He emphasized the need for religion to be practical and integrated into all aspects of our lives. The central message of Vedanta is the idea of oneness, and that our human purpose is to realize our divine nature. Through his teachings on the four yogas, the harmony of religions, the divinity of the soul, and serving humanity as God, Vivekananda provided spiritual seekers with paths to that realization. He also made significant contributions to both world culture and Indian culture.
